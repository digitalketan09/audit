Your headline positions you as an observer — not a future pediatric oncologist
Your current headline reads: "Observing patient care with cultural sensitivity and awareness." The single most important real-estate on your entire profile is describing you as observing — passive, peripheral, outside the action. That's the first thing every recruiter, program coordinator, and fellow doctor sees.

Your About states clearly that you're pursuing pediatric oncology or neonatology. None of that appears in your headline, your experience titles, or your featured content. The headline is doing the opposite of what your credentials and ambition justify.
What this costs you Every time someone searches for "pediatric resident," "neonatology," "IMG," or "Yenepoya" — you don't surface. And when they do land on your profile, the first sentence asks them to picture you watching, not practicing.
🔗
You've never actually claimed your LinkedIn identity
Your profile URL ends in /sha5ku1fu6si — the random suffix LinkedIn auto-generates for accounts that were never customized. It's a small thing, but it's a signal. Every serious doctor, every program director, every journalist who looks you up sees the same thing: this person hasn't treated their LinkedIn as an owned professional asset.

Small structural signals like this compound with the rest of the profile. Your name, your specialty keywords, your search surface — none of it is working in your favor right now.
What this costs you Searchability. Credibility-at-a-glance. A thousand tiny compound moments where someone almost takes you seriously and then decides you're not ready.
🔄
You're amplifying everyone else's voice — almost never your own
In the last 90 days, your activity shows at least 7 reposts of other people's content (Kenny Gibson, WHO, Australian Indigenous Doctors' Association) and only 2 original posts — one about First Nations racism, one about fear/mindset using a children's movie metaphor. Neither is anchored to pediatric oncology, neonatology, or anything that signals your clinical identity.

The algorithm reads this pattern and concludes: consumer. Not contributor. And when a peer or a selector visits your profile, they see a doctor who shares what other people say — not someone building a voice of her own in the field she actually wants to lead in.
What this costs you You're training LinkedIn — and everyone who sees it — to associate your name with other people's authority, not yours. Every repost without an original next to it widens that gap.

Why This Is Actually Hard
Knowing the gaps is the easy part
Most doctors who read an audit like this nod, agree with every point, open a new tab to start fixing the headline — and stop within 10 days. Not because they weren't motivated. Because the execution side of this, while working 60-hour clinical weeks, is genuinely harder than it looks from the outside.
🕐
The Cadence Problem
LinkedIn doesn't reward one great post. It rewards 40 good ones across 6 months. Inside a hospital rotation, finding the time and headspace to ship that consistently — week after week, including the weeks you're post-nights and exhausted — is where 90% of doctors quietly stop.
🧠
The Voice Problem
Writing in a way that sounds like a credible clinician — not a medical student, not a motivational account — while also being readable to non-doctors (selectors, journalists, program coordinators) is a craft. It takes most people 30–50 posts of trial and error to find it. That's 12 months on your own.
🔍
The Angle Problem
What should a pediatric-oncology-bound IMG actually post about? Cases (with ethics boundaries)? Papers? Reflections? Global health? The answer isn't obvious, and picking wrong burns goodwill that's hard to rebuild. Most doctors either post too clinically (boring) or too generally (forgettable).
🤝
The Network Problem
Adding 1,000 strangers is easy. Building relationships with the 50 pediatric oncology and neonatology leaders whose attention actually matters in 3 years — and showing up in their feed consistently enough to be remembered — is a different game. Cold adds don't work for this. A system does.
📉
The Algorithm Penalty
You're in a decay cycle right now. The longer a profile goes without original content, the less reach each new post gets — which demoralizes people into posting even less. Reversing the decay is technical, not motivational. You can't "try harder" your way out of it.
⚖️
The Identity Problem
Shifting your online identity from "observing" to "practicing" without sounding arrogant, overclaiming, or cringe is the hardest part for early-career doctors. It's a judgment call on every single post. Most IMGs get this wrong in one direction or the other for their entire first year of trying.
