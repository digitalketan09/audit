🔖
Your headline is a credentials list, not a claim of authority
Your current headline reads: "Functional Medicine, Hormonal & Fertility Expert LAc. HOM MSTCM" — three niches and three credentials, stacked. It tells a visitor what you have. It doesn't tell them who you help, how, or what only you can say.

The strongest positioning lines in your field don't lead with credentials. They lead with the specific problem the practitioner is known for solving. Yours is doing the opposite — asking the visitor to do the interpretive work of figuring out which kind of expert you are, on which kind of case.
What this costs you When benefits directors, journalists, or peer practitioners scan you in a search, there's no single sentence that tells them "this is the woman who does that kind of work." You get remembered as "a functional medicine expert" — a category, not a person.
🔗
Your LinkedIn URL still ends in /52822150
Your profile URL ends in /anga-gonzalez-52822150 — the auto-generated numeric suffix LinkedIn assigns to accounts that haven't customized their vanity URL. For a 20-year practitioner with a platform-grade affiliation, this is the smallest possible signal that LinkedIn isn't being treated as the professional asset it deserves to be.

Every time a journalist, patient, or corporate partner Googles your name, the URL they land on telegraphs "account, not brand." It's fixable in 30 seconds — and exactly the kind of detail that signals how the rest of the profile is being treated.
What this costs you Trust-at-a-glance. Referral shares that get copy-pasted into team chats. Every tiny compounding moment where someone expected a polished personal brand and found a placeholder URL instead.
🕳️
Your Posts tab literally says "Nothing to see for now"
When a visitor clicks "Posts" under your activity, LinkedIn returns: "Nothing to see for now. Articles, posts, documents, images, videos, or events that Anga adds will appear here."

Meanwhile your recent activity is filled with reposts of Jacqueline L., Dr. David Reiner, Joanna Krzeslak-Hoogland, Deanna Minich, James M. K., and Nova Dietetics — all smart, all relevant, none of them you. The algorithm reads this pattern and classifies you as an amplifier, not an author. Your 4,486 followers opened their feed today, saw a post you shared from someone else, and walked away having absorbed their ideas — not yours.
What this costs you The single biggest underleverage in your profile: 4,486 people signed up to hear from you, and the official public record shows zero original thoughts. Twenty years of clinical observations, patient patterns, and hard-won insight — sitting silent while your follower count grows on other people's content.
📝
Your About opens with "two decades of enriching experience"…and truncates
The visible opening of your About section reads: "With over two decades of enriching experience, my journey as a Chinese Medicine Practitioner has bee…" and collapses into a "see more" before the first real claim is made.

The first 220 characters of an About section are the only part most visitors ever read. Yours opens with a phrase that could describe any practitioner with 20 years of experience in any modality. There is no hook, no case, no unusual angle that would make a Stork Club HR director, a fertility journalist, or a peer practitioner pause and read further. The depth is inside — but the surface gives them no reason to dig.
What this costs you Most profile visits convert to memory or they don't. An About that opens with a generic decades-of-experience phrase gets remembered as "experienced" — a pleasant, forgettable category. Twenty years of rare clinical practice deserves a first line that closes loops, not one that opens a category.
🎯
Fertility, hormonal, and functional — three niches, no lane
Your headline claims three expertise areas simultaneously: functional medicine, hormonal health, and fertility. All three are genuine, all three are related, and all three are different LinkedIn audiences with different feeds, different interests, and different decision-makers.

A corporate benefits director searching "fertility specialist" filters differently than a peri-menopause wellness editor searching "hormonal health" or a functional-medicine clinic seeking a contributing practitioner. Right now your profile sits in the intersection of all three — and because it doesn't lead in any one, it doesn't rank top-of-mind in any one. The positioning isn't wrong; it's unpicked.
What this costs you Partial authority in three adjacent niches instead of dominant authority in the one that actually drives your best opportunities. The next Stork Club-tier partnership, the next JAMA Integrative piece, the next keynote — they go to the practitioner who owns a lane, not the one who names three.
🧩
The scattered playbook: five parallel tactics, no system
Website. Video content made with a friend. Instagram Lives for Q&A. An ebook to write. Courses to build. Monthly blogging. Each one of these is a reasonable visibility tactic on its own. Run in parallel, by one person, inside a full clinical week — they are six separate projects, each under-resourced, none compounding into the others.

The core issue isn't motivation or ideas. It's that every one of those tactics is a standalone effort. The website doesn't feed the LinkedIn presence. The videos don't distribute to LinkedIn. The blog doesn't loop into the ebook. The ebook doesn't promote through the courses. Six engines running in isolation — and one practitioner alternating between them in the cracks between patient sessions.
What this costs you Every hour you spend on one of them moves the other five backwards. A year from now, the audience that existed today is still 4,486 — because none of the six tactics was given the concentrated output required to compound.
📹
The content exists — it's just not on LinkedIn
You've mentioned filming video content with a friend. That work is real — and it's the single most valuable raw material for LinkedIn visibility a practitioner in your space can have. But none of it is surfacing on your profile. No native video posts. No Featured section entries linking to it. No transcriptions turned into text posts. No clips reshared for the LinkedIn feed.

That means the effort is happening. The output is being produced. And the 4,486 people who follow you on LinkedIn are unaware any of it exists. The content-production problem is already solved. The distribution-to-LinkedIn problem hasn't been touched.
What this costs you The steepest form of wasted effort: work you've already done, which your existing audience never gets to see. Every video lost to a single platform is a week of leverage left on the table.
📆
Monday is admin day — not a visibility day
You see patients the rest of the week. Monday is your catch-up day for meetings, admin, and CEU study. That's an honest calendar. It's also the exact schedule where a visibility effort — if it depends on "finding time" inside Monday — will lose to admin 48 weeks of the year.

Every practitioner who tries to build LinkedIn visibility inside a single weekly gap ends up in one of two places: the content stops mid-month when one Monday gets consumed by something urgent, or the visibility work steals from CEU study time and the studying stops. Neither outcome is acceptable. And neither is solved by willpower — it's a structural problem about where the content production actually happens.
What this costs you The exhaustion of trying to will a visibility rhythm into existence on top of a full clinical schedule — and the slow resignation that follows when Monday after Monday gets taken by something else.

Why This Is Actually Hard
Knowing the gaps is the easy part
Most practitioners who read an audit like this nod, agree with every point, open a new tab to rewrite their headline — and stop within 10 days. Not because they weren't motivated. Because the execution side of this, while running a clinical practice inside a platform company, is genuinely harder than it looks from the outside.
🌀
The Pivot Problem
You've trained your audience, your algorithm, and your own habit loop to expect curation. Shifting from amplifier to voice isn't a one-post decision — it's a months-long re-signal that initially under-performs before it compounds. Most practitioners quit during the under-performing phase.
🗣️
The Voice Problem
Translating 20 years of Chinese-medicine clinical nuance into LinkedIn-readable posts — without flattening it into generic wellness content, and without alienating the peer practitioners who'll smell oversimplification — is a craft. It takes most people 30–50 iterations to find their register. That's 12 months on your own.
🎯
The Niche Problem
Functional. Hormonal. Fertility. Picking the lane that becomes your public identity — while the other two support it rather than compete with it — is a strategic call, not a preference call. Get it wrong and you rebuild the audience from scratch a year later. Get it right and every post compounds.
🕐
The Cadence Problem
LinkedIn doesn't reward one great post. It rewards 40 good ones across six months. Inside a Stork Club workload plus private-practice patients, finding the time and headspace to ship that consistently — week after week, including the weeks you're clinic-exhausted — is where 90% of practitioners quietly stop.
📉
The Algorithm Penalty
You're currently classified as a curator account — high repost ratio, low original signal. That classification decays original-post reach when you do publish. Reversing it is technical, not motivational. You can't "try harder" your way out of an algorithm that has categorized you.
⚖️
The Authority Paradox
Twenty-year practitioners often lose LinkedIn visibility to social-first newcomers with a third of the expertise but a tenth of the caution. Your clinical restraint — which makes you trustworthy in practice — can make you invisible on a platform that rewards opinion. Threading that needle without becoming the thing you don't want to become is the hardest piece of the whole thing.
